Writing for Web Accessibility

Learn how to write web content that is accessible to all users, including people with disabilities.

Key Writing Principles

Provide Informative Headings

Use clear, descriptive headings to organize content. Good headings help all users, especially those using screen readers, to navigate and understand your content structure.

Keep Content Clear and Concise

Write in simple, straightforward language. Avoid jargon and complex sentences when possible. This helps users with cognitive disabilities and benefits all readers.

Structure Your Content

Use short paragraphs, bulleted lists, and meaningful subheadings. Well-structured content is easier to scan and understand.

Write Meaningful Link Text

Avoid "click here" or "read more." Instead, use descriptive link text that makes sense out of context, like "Download 2024 Accessibility Report (PDF, 2MB)".

Best Practices Examples

Instead of:

"Click here to learn more about our services"

Write:

"Explore our web development and accessibility consulting services"

Instead of:

"Recent research shows that..."

Write:

"A 2024 WebAIM study found that 96% of home pages had detectable accessibility errors"

Additional Guidelines

Alternative Text

Provide descriptive alternative text for images that convey information. Decorative images can have empty alt attributes.

Tables

Use tables only for tabular data. Include proper headers and captions to make them accessible to screen reader users.

Document Language

Specify the primary language of your content and indicate language changes within the content when they occur.

Based on W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) guidelines. For complete documentation, visit the official W3C WAI website.
